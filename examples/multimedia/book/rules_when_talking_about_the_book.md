# Rules for Writing About This Investigation

## Audience Awareness

- **Assume expert readers**: The target audience may have more documents, archives, and knowledge than you. Write for people who know the subject deeply.
- **Don't over-explain known context**: Historical background they already know wastes their time and signals you don't understand your audience.
- **Order information strategically**: Prioritize elements relevant to the specific audience (language order, credential emphasis, etc.).

## Language and Assertions

- **No hard assertions about historical events**: Use "allegedly", "presumably", "possibly", "the investigation suggests", "may have", "avrebbe".
- **No claims about what documents do or don't explain**: Their archives may contain documents you haven't seen.
- **No claims of uniqueness or priority**: Never say "first to investigate", "no one has asked", "never before explored". Someone may have.
- **State caveats once**: Acknowledge limitations (e.g., "based on archives accessible to me") once at the beginning, then write naturally without repetition.

## Scope and Humility

- **Scope claims to what you can deliver**: "This book breaks my family's silence" not "this book breaks the silence".
- **Present work without comparison**: Don't diminish others' work to elevate your own. Don't claim gaps in existing research—present your contribution on its own merits.
- **Be precise about status**: If something is "coming soon", say so. Don't overstate readiness.

## Institutional Respect

- **Never criticize established research institutions**: Reference their work respectfully. Position your work as complementary, not corrective.
- **Acknowledge the field**: Your work builds on existing scholarship, not despite it.

## Content Discipline

- **Keep narrative substance**: When softening assertions, change tone—don't remove content.
- **Simplify where possible**: Complex explanations (genealogy, technical processes) should be reduced to their clearest form.
- **No redundancy**: State important points once, clearly. Repetition signals uncertainty.

## Strategic Disclosure

- **Protect sensitive findings**: Some discoveries are disclosed strategically over time. Never reveal more than intended for the current context.
- **No specific identifiers without authorization**: Archive IDs, document numbers, and certain names may be withheld for future disclosure.
- **Subtle hints, not explicit claims**: When referencing undisclosed findings, trigger interest without revealing specifics.

## Professional Terminology

- **Use field-appropriate language**: "Intelligence analysis, historical research, and investigative journalism"—not casual terms.
- **Match register to context**: Academic submissions require academic tone. Editorial pitches require editorial tone.

## Core Principle

**Write as if the reader knows more than you, has access to more than you, and will reject anything that sounds uninformed, arrogant, or imprecise.**
